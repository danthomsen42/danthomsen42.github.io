Navigation Here

Here, in the Navigation area, I am demonstrating what using desendent selectors do and don't do.
As you can see, "Back to Home" is in a box all on its own. Now here is an example of the CSS code:
nav ul p{
background-color: aqua;
padding: 5px;
border: 5px dashed #000000;
margin: 5px;
width: 200px;
}
Notice how the definition of the CSS includes "nav", "ul" and "p" are one after the other. That says
that only "p" individuals who are desendants of "ul" who are desendants of "nav" will recieve this
specific treatment. "Back to Home" meets those requirements, where as "Homework Links" do not.

Pictures

Now Look at these pictures with their wonderful borders. Notice how the one with Link and the Dog,
and the Pug Fellowship both have green borders around them, and the Speeder Cats have a different
red border. That is because the green bordered pictures are identified with the same attribute.
Example codes:
img[alt="cat"]{
background-color: brown;
padding: 7px;
border: 5px dotted #010100;
width: 500px;
}
img[alt="dog"]{
padding:10px;
background-color: chartreuse;
border: ridge #009900;
border-width: 10px;
}
Both are identified with the "alt" attribute of "dog" as opposed to "cat". Attributes are used
to identify specific "items" with a name or type. That way those things with the same attributes
will have the same characteristics.

Lyrics

Now Children, are perhaps one of the most picky of these Selectors. They only apply
to the direct desendants of these "items" and to nothing and no one else.
Example:

main>ul>li>ul{
background-color: blueviolet;
border: 5px dashed #809900;
padding: 5px;
margin: 5px;
width: 200px;
}
As you can see, only those very specific lines are shaded, and not any of the others.
They are easily identifiable by ">" down the "ancestral line".
